Everyone wants a number. There isn't one, and anybody who gives you a flat figure without asking what you sell is guessing.

But there is a way to work it out from your own numbers in about ten minutes, and it is far more useful than a benchmark you found in a blog post written for a different country.

## Start from what a customer is worth, not from what you can spare

Most businesses set an ad budget by asking "what can we afford to lose?" That framing guarantees the number is too small to learn anything from.

Work forward instead.

**1. What is one customer worth to you?**

Not the transaction — the customer. If a dental patient books a ₱9,000 package and typically returns twice more over two years, the customer is worth considerably more than ₱9,000. If a condo unit earns you a commission, that commission is the number, not the unit price.

**2. What are you willing to pay to acquire one?**

This is a business decision, not a marketing one. Many service businesses can comfortably pay 10–20% of first-purchase value to acquire a customer. High-margin, high-repeat categories can pay far more. Low-margin retail can pay far less.

**3. How many do you want per month?**

Be realistic about capacity. There is no point generating forty inquiries a week if you can only service ten.

Multiply steps two and three and you have a starting monthly budget. That is your acquisition budget — the amount you are prepared to hand to Meta to bring you customers.

## Then sanity-check it against the learning problem

Here is where most small budgets fail, and it is mechanical rather than philosophical.

Meta's delivery system needs a certain volume of the event you are optimising for before it can reliably find more of those people. Below that volume it never really exits the learning phase, and performance stays unstable and expensive.

The practical consequence: a budget that is too small does not produce proportionally smaller results. It often produces *no reliable results at all*, because the system never gets enough signal to learn from. Spending ₱5,000 a month is not one-tenth as effective as spending ₱50,000. It can be zero.

So the second question is not "can I afford this?" but "is this enough to generate enough conversion events for the system to learn?" If the honest answer is no, you have two options: optimise for a cheaper, more frequent event higher in the funnel, or wait until you can fund it properly.

## Ad spend is not the same as agency fees

Worth stating plainly because it causes a lot of confusion and some genuinely bad deals.

**Ad spend** is what you pay Meta. It buys the impressions.

**Management fee** is what you pay whoever runs the campaigns. It buys the strategy, the creative, the testing and the reporting.

These should be separate, and the ad account should be yours. If an agency bundles them into one number and will not tell you the split, you cannot tell whether your money bought advertising or overhead. You should also be able to log into the ad account and see the spend yourself, at any time, without asking.

## Common mistakes that make budget irrelevant

**Spreading it too thin.** Five campaigns on small budgets all fail to learn. One campaign properly funded has a chance.

**Changing it constantly.** Every significant budget change can reset the learning phase. Pick a number, give it a few weeks.

**Not accounting for the offer.** No budget saves a weak offer. If your price, positioning or landing experience is the problem, more spend buys you more evidence of the problem.

**Forgetting the response cost.** Budget for answering the inquiries. Ads that generate messages nobody replies to are the most expensive thing you can buy.

## A reasonable starting posture

If you are beginning, fund one clear campaign, with one clear objective, at a level that can plausibly generate enough conversions to learn from, for a minimum of three months. Three months is not an upsell — it is roughly what it takes to get through learning, gather real data, and act on it.

Then judge it on cost per qualified inquiry and what those inquiries closed at. Not on reach. Not on likes.
