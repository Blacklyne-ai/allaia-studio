If you run a skin clinic, dental practice or wellness studio, you have almost certainly had an ad rejected with an unhelpful message and no obvious explanation. Then you edited something at random, resubmitted, and it passed — or it didn't, and you gave up.

Health and aesthetics is one of the most restricted categories on the platform. The rules are largely knowable, and building creative around them from the start is much cheaper than appealing rejections afterwards.

## The rule that catches people most often

Meta restricts ads that imply knowledge of, or draw attention to, a person's **personal attributes** — including physical characteristics and health conditions.

This is why copy addressed directly at a perceived flaw gets rejected. Phrasing like "Are you struggling with acne?" or "Tired of your double chin?" implies you know something personal about the viewer.

The fix is usually a change of grammar rather than a change of message. Describe the treatment and the outcome rather than addressing the reader's assumed problem:

- Not: *"Got dark underarms? We can fix it."*
- Instead: *"Underarm brightening treatment, from ₱X per session."*

Same offer. One implies you have assessed the reader. The other describes a service.

## Before-and-after imagery

This is the second big one, and the rules are stricter than most clinics expect. Direct before-and-after comparisons showing dramatic results are heavily restricted, as is imagery focusing on an isolated body part or implying an unrealistic outcome.

Safer approaches:

- Show the treatment being performed, in a clean, professional setting
- Show the environment, the equipment and the team
- Use a single image rather than a split comparison
- Focus on the experience rather than the transformation

Yes, before-and-afters convert. They also get accounts restricted, and a restricted account costs far more than a slightly weaker creative.

## Claims you cannot support

Anything absolute is a risk: *permanent*, *guaranteed*, *cure*, *100% safe*, *no side effects*. Beyond platform rules, these create real exposure with health regulators and with patients.

Keep claims specific and defensible. "Five sessions" is a fact. "Guaranteed results" is a promise you cannot keep.

## Practical things that reduce rejections

**Have the page and business set up properly.** A thin, brand-new page advertising medical treatments attracts more scrutiny than an established one.

**Do not repeatedly resubmit the same rejected creative.** Repeated violations affect account standing. Fix the underlying issue, or appeal once with a clear explanation.

**Keep the landing experience consistent.** If your ad is compliant and the page it leads to is full of miracle claims, that mismatch is itself a problem.

**Build a small bank of pre-approved creative.** Once you know which formats pass reliably, produce variations on those rather than reinventing each month.

## The bigger point

It is tempting to treat the rules as an obstacle between you and the ad you want to run. In a category that lives on trust, they are closer to a floor.

Patients choosing a clinic are making a decision about safety and competence. Advertising that overclaims does short-term damage to conversion and long-term damage to reputation. The clinics that do well over years tend to be the ones whose advertising sounds like a professional practice rather than a discount sale.

Compliant creative is not weaker creative. It is usually just more specific.
